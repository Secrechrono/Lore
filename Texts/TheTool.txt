Magic, as all you now know, carries a debt written not in coin, nor in time,
but in blood: warm, pulsing, and unwilling.And it was I who carved that truth

into the marrow of the world. Not from malice, nor cruelty, but from a cold, radiant clarity: that blood alone could bind life to the arcane in its heat and hue, in its grief and gift, lay the perfect conduit between soul and spell.

So I etched it into the weft of reality.


I made it law.
Every charm must bleed.
Every rune must pulse.
Every miracle must have a cost.


And if one dared to weave against death itself 
then the thread must be dyed red.

Yes, I needed blood, for I sought to challenge
even the silence of the grave.
If I was to split the heavens, to unmake the veil,
then my hands must soak in crimson.



Yet blood is never rare in this world 
not where pride festers,
not where kings rise high on thrones of ivory
and fall to steel and flame.


Where vanity blooms unchecked,
where gold gleams too brightly,
where chalices overflow and children scream 
there, always, I found the crimson I required.

It was not wrath, nor love, but desire that guided me.
Desire: the mortal fire that births both glory and ruin.
And if one seeks blood, one need only follow
where mortals long most deeply.

Thus I was led not by compass, nor omen,
but by hunger and war, by ambition and loss 
through battlefields strewn with the fallen,
harvesting bodies by the dozen,
and still…
not enough.
Not enough to break the sky.



I was no harvester, no crude reaper of men 
I was a weaver, a spinner of laws and longing,
one who shaped purpose from pain
and rhythm from ruin.
I did not butcher. I did not steal.
I created.



From the red desire that dripped beneath mortal crowns,
from silent prayers whispered behind golden doors,
I gathered threads — words, runes, hungers —
and spun them into a vessel.

A doll, if you must name it so.
But carved with sentences of pride,
and marks of love for one's own reflection.
Each glyph a secret I’d learned from watching kingdoms crumble.
Each sigil, a breath stolen from ambition’s final gasp.

In that boy that husk of spellbound bone and sorrowed flesh 
I poured the world’s vanity.


Its pride.
Its radiant hunger to be more than it was.

He was not born.
He was woven.
Not of womb, but of wound.
And when he opened his eyes, he did not cry.
He bled.
His golden locks turned red.

And when the spellweaver — I — ran dry,
I clawed across his eyes.


His blood spilled onto threads spun
from the world’s own desire —
a thing I still, to this day, admire.
He — the boy — was a blood pool,
my precious blonde tool.

For he did not dream, neither did he die.
Yet one day…
he asked me:
Father... why?


His blood was spilling behind his cries.

I had birthed a god of desire.
And so, in mercy, I sent him away 
to grow humble, free from pain.
Erythremir, I called him not.

He chose that name,
from what I believe…
was spite.

My second child I abandoned, perhaps this was a taller sin than using him for his blood, so as I had carved my claws in him so did he carve his name on this world, the Lord of Crimson now roams.
