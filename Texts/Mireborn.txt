Oh, Erythremir//verily, he was perfection made manifest; the very breath of the divine wrought in flesh. Through unnumbered failures and the ruin of my lesser works did I at last lay claw upon his splendor. The world itself did hunger for him, yet his strength burned too bright for any vessel of thread to bear. Had I but tasted of his blood, I might have woven a form to endure such radiance//yet alas, it was denied me. Thus was I driven to seek another path by which to summon him into being. Stagnate, my firstborn aye, she was but a means unto an end… nay, unto many ends.

Stagnate, witch of sloth, so named by her kin, for never did she stir nor draw breath in haste. Her stillness was no grace of serenity, but the fetter of her own making; the threads that held her form did writhe with ceaseless torment. Her flesh was marred, her spirit more so twisted beneath the weight of my design. All she could do was linger in silence, to watch as my craft took shape before her hollow gaze; and through that watching, she learned.

Never had I need of a witch that mantle she claimed of her own accord. All I required was a broodmother.

Scales of the dragon, eyes of the serpent, and the silken flesh of the moth—such were the makings of her frame. Her body was no birth of nature’s will, but a design most deliberate, an amalgam of all that this weary world did yield of worth. My first daughter, she was forged not to live, but to bear to cradle the seed of those yet unshaped, the children that were to come.



Her firstborn an abortive thing, a worthless mote of flesh was naught but failure. With a hand grown cruel by long endeavour I dashed its head upon the stones of that hollow cave; she could neither wail nor stay my hand, for her limbs were spent and her vigil already given o'er to the forging of the next attempt at Erythremir. I rent and stilled many more; countless whelps I crushed beneath tooth and claw, and swallowed what remained for in their marrow the sorcery ran thick and potent.

She could but watch, bereft of power, as I fed upon her miscreations; and from that ravenous feast I drew strength to weave anew. What pity there might have been was long since braided into my purpose, and mercy lay smothered beneath the ash of intent.



Till one fateful day she brought forth a creature unlike all before a wretch of myriad visages, a twisted colossus wrought in error and agony, twice my stature and thrice my dread. Its form was a tangle of faces, each whispering the echoes of those I had slain. Yet though it towered above me, it quailed, and hid itself behind her broken frame.

And lo//she rose. For the first time since her making, Stagnate stood. The threads that had long shackled her did tremble and sing, as though the very weave of my craft rebelled. In her eyes, no longer dim with submission, there burned a spark most terrible a mother’s defiance, born of pain unending.



“Spare them i beg of you, Cease this endless feast”, her hands trembled yet she stood

I answered their marrow feeds my mistakes, perfects the weave, gets closer to Erythremir

Yet she turned back to that wretched being, as she pressed her forhead upon one of the many she had birthed, kinship that knew no bounds



“False, I’ll name this one false, let it live and you can eat me”



Out of a mercy long tarnished I yielded. When her travail failed, I made of her flesh a feast; each miscarriage I devoured, and in their marrow the loom found new cunning. Her wits unspooled beneath the unending torment yet in that unravelling there grew a strange devotion: she did love the pain, as some worship flame. Thus she learned to beg for the blade as for benediction, and I maker and butcher both kept faith with that cruel covenant.



The pleading for pain for my claws to pierce her flesh this was never woven into her design. It was a madness born of suffering too deep for mortal measure. When I denied her, she would turn upon herself, rending her own womb to loose the unborn before their time. The cave would echo with the sound of torn thread and shattered bone.

In those moments, I beheld something that should not have been: a will beyond my making. Stagnate, my stillborn muse, my wretched vessel she frightened me. For in her brokenness, she had found a power that no craft of mine could command.



Until that day, the day Erythremir came forth he tore through her womb in a storm of blood and light, his birth a sundering of flesh and fate alike. I drove my blade into his breast, that I might see if his veins ran endless as i needed, and she did not move to stay my hand. Instead—

“Look, little one,” she whispered, her voice soft as unraveling silk, “Father loves thee, as he loves me.”

He was her light, her redemption made flesh. And the boy—blind in his innocence grew to think that monstrous woman his sister. How I loathed the bond that bloomed between them, a thread spun beyond my weaving. It bound them with something no craft of mine could sever love, that ancient heresy.

So I freed Erythremir from her shadow, unbound him from her touch, and cast Stagnate and her broods into exile. She had served her purpose. I had no need of her anymore.
