It was night, as it always seemed to be, and once again I was plagued by terrible visions. They were a common occurrence in my youth back when I was building the cave. The stress was relentless, always watching for demons, always fearing for what lay above. But I had done it I had carved out a place where my wife and I could live, a place we hoped would one day echo with our children’s laughter.



On nights like this, when the visions returned, she would hold me close, her embrace steadying the chaos in my mind. Without a word, she would remove her necklace and place it gently around my neck. That simple, intimate gesture paired with her soft-spoken reassurances always brought me back. Always kept me from waking our daughter with my cries..



Perhaps those visions were more than memories perhaps they were warnings.



It happened on one of those nights.



I woke drenched in sweat, the sheets clinging to me. My wife was no longer by my side. I heard crying soft, broken coming from our daughter’s room. I rushed toward it, my legs moving faster than my thoughts, my heart pounding in my throat.



And then I saw it.



A creature darker than the night itself loomed in the shadows. It had thirty feet, each twisted into a different grotesque shape. Its body was a mass of wrongness, and its three mouths goatlike, reeking, unnerving chewed with slow, deliberate rhythm.



It stood over my daughter’s crib.



And it was feeding//



feeding not on flesh, but on fear.



It wasn’t devouring our daughter. No. It was making her watch. Forcing her to look upon her mother’s mutilated corpse.



My wife’s corpse.



And then the creature noticed me.



That devil...did the same with Aisha..my...poor daughter, as soon as I caught it's eye...it started to chew on my baby



Yet when it approached me while I stood frozen in terror it didn’t kill me.



At first, I thought it was cruelty. That it wanted to savor my fear, laugh at my helplessness. But then… it recoiled. The creature let out a screeching wail, a sound like rusted metal dragged across stone. It was in pain.



Because of the necklace.



My wife’s necklace still warm against my chest, still glowing faintly where it rested at my throat.



And in that moment, I killed it.



With my bare hands.



Not out of courage, but rage.



Rage not at the monster, but at myself. At my weakness. At the fact that I had the necklace, and not her.



Because if she had been the one to wake…

She would have reached our daughter in time.

She would have saved her.



But instead it was me.

I picked up my hammer and walked to the forge.



I had never crafted a weapon before but I would now. I had to. If I was to erase the monsters from this world, it would begin with fire and steel.



So I began to shape metal.



Days bled into decades, decades into centuries. I hammered without rest. I forged weapons for others, for warriors, for the lost, for the vengeful. I spread them across Erev like seeds of retribution.



This became my life: the rhythmic strike of hammer to anvil, the soft hiss of quenched iron, and the silence between it all where memory still lived. My hair turned grey at the forge, but my purpose never dulled.



They began to call me Theleas of the Forge.



Later, Theleas the Grandmaster.



Titles piled upon me like the ashes of the past but none could return what I had lost. My kin, my wife, my child… were gone.



Still, dwarves came.



One by one, then by the dozens, they sought me out. A society began to form around the forge’s fires. They asked who should lead once I was gone.



I answered simply:

"The strongest smith."



Because the dwarves needed strength.



Strength to survive. Strength to remember.

And strength to never again be caught sleeping.



One day, a man arrived followed by a spirit and a dragon.



He spoke of rekindled skies, of hope, of peace. The kind of bullshit not even a mead-drunk fool would ramble. But I'd given my weapons to bigger idiots than him. It didn’t matter what they believed only what they killed. As long as demons fell, I didn’t care if they thought stars would sing them lullabies.



But this man… surprised me.



He stepped into my forge not to demand a blade, nor axe, nor spear.



He asked for something else.



By then, I was carved with scars, half-blind in one eye, and just beginning to grey. But I heard him clearly.



“A ring,” he said. “Or maybe a necklace. I don’t know what would suit her.”



I blinked at him. “A ring? You wish to rekindle life and you come to a forge asking for a trinket? Not a weapon?”



He looked at me, bold and certain. “A weapon will never express love. And to rekindle a flame… I must first rekindle some smiles.”



“I lost my family to the night,” I said, the words dragging out like rusted chains. “I live because of this necklace. If I’d had a weapon... I could’ve protected them. My wife wouldn’t have had to give me this. She’d be alive. I’d be the one dead.”



My voice broke. The fire behind me hissed, as if it too couldn’t bear the weight of the truth.



The man looked at me quiet, not pitying, just listening.



Then he smiled, soft and sincere.

“Then she must have really loved you,” he said.



“I’d like a ring like that,” he said.



Then, without a word, he stepped forward and hugged me.



A grown man hugging me.



And instead of shoving him off in fury, I... I broke.



Tears welled up before I could stop them. Because in that moment, I didn’t see some fool with dreams I saw a man trying to build a home, for his beloved. Not just for the joyous spirit he called his wife, but for something greater.



He loved all of creation. That was clear.



And something in me something long buried moved.



I decided then that I’d follow him for a while. Just long enough to finish the ring. Just long enough to make sure he, his spirit, and even that damned dragon made it through.



And perhaps just perhaps those days spent not making weapons...



Were the last time I truly felt alive.



Since the day I last held my daughter.
